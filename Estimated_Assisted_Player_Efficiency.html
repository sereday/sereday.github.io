Estimating Efficiencies for Created and Assisted Shots

A central debate in basketball analytics is the weight of efficiency versus production. While it is often assumed that players who shoot more are more efficient, regression analysis reveals a slight negative correlation between field goal attempts per 100 possessions and effective field goal percentage (eFG%).

[Image of regression scatter plot showing the negative correlation between FGA/100 and eFG%]

The Difficulty of the Unassisted Shot

As a player's shot volume increases, the average difficulty of those shots rises. Teams are happy to let any player take an open layup, but only a few are permitted to regularly take contested unassisted jumpers. Data shows that an assist-type pass creates a significantly better scoring opportunity than a self-created attempt.

Key Observation: The largest regression coefficient belongs to created 2-point jump shots. Players who can maintain efficiency while increasing these difficult attempts provide the most value to an offense's "ceiling."

In summary, while all shot types show a positive relationship between attempts and expected points, the rate of diminishing returns is much steeper for unassisted shots than for those created by teammates.

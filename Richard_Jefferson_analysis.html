Should We Expect a Bounce-Back Year from Richard Jefferson?

The Spurs acquired Richard Jefferson before the 2009-10 season in hopes of supplementing Tim Duncan, Manu Ginobili, and Tony Parker. By paying him like an All-Star, the expectation was that he would absorb a significant portion of the scoring load. After averaging nearly 20 points per game the previous year, expectations were high for Jefferson to become one of the most potent fourth options in the NBA.

However, Jefferson averaged just over 12 points in his first season with San Antonio. As we look toward 2011, there is noticeable optimism that a second year in the system will yield significant improvement.

The Statistical Reality of the Drop

There were several indicators that a decrease in scoring was inevitable given the change in environment. The Spurs were a superior team to the Milwaukee Bucks, leading to a reduction in Jefferson's minutes (from 36 to 31 MPG) and fewer shot attempts as he shared the floor with better alternatives.

Projected vs. Actual Production:
  • Initial Expectation: Accounting for age and minutes, a player with Jefferson’s 2009 production would be expected to average 16.4 PPG in 2010.
  • The "New Team" Adjustment: Players switching to quality teams typically see an additional drop; for Jefferson, this adjusted the expectation to 16 PPG.

Key Case Studies

Ray Allen (2007-08): Allen knowingly accepted a reduced role alongside Garnett and Pierce. Because his role was fundamentally redefined, it was no surprise that his scoring did not "bounce back" the following year.
Jason Terry (2004-05): Terry represents the ideal "bounce-back" scenario. After struggling to adjust in year one (12.4 PPG), he improved to 17 PPG in his second season with Dallas.
Rashard Lewis (2007-08): Lewis showed a more typical trajectory. While his raw scoring didn't jump in year two, his efficiency (TSA/48) increased significantly as he acclimated to the Magic’s system.

2011 Outlook: Reasons for Optimism

While Jefferson’s Offensive Rating remained steady from 2009 to 2010, his diminished role should have resulted in an efficiency improvement of about five points per 100 possessions. As he moves into 2011, there are early signs that he is rediscovering his aggressive edge.

Areas for Improvement:

  • Aggression: In early 2011 play, Jefferson is averaging 6.6 free throws per 36 minutes, a figure much closer to his peak years.
  • 3-Point Usage: Increasing his 3-point frequency (which was 50% higher in 2009) would benefit the Spurs’ spacing.
  • Off-Screen Sets: Jefferson was only utilized on 67 "Off-Screen" possessions last year, compared to approximately 250 in prior efficient years.

While a total Jason Terry-style return to form is unlikely, the data suggests a modest but meaningful increase in Richard Jefferson’s contributions for the 2011 season.

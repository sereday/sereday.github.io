Dynamic Replacement Level

Most metrics assume that if a star is injured, they are replaced by a single fringe player. In reality, a star's absence leads to a redistribution of minutes and shots across the entire roster[cite: 9].

The Assumption

My model assumes that when a starter goes down, the 2nd-string player takes half the vacated minutes while the other starters make up the rest. This cascading effect continues down to the 3rd string and eventually to a fringe player[cite: 9].

Case Study: Pau Gasol (2009/10)

Using the Cascade model, we can calculate Gasol's dynamic replacement levels[cite: 9]:

  • Expected Replacement Level: -2.81[cite: 9]
  • Mean Expected Replacement Level: -3.04[cite: 9]

By using this approach, we account for the fact that better teams—like the Lakers—tend to have more capable replacement players than lottery teams[cite: 9].
